Murray-Darling Basin · Australia

Australia's Food Bowl
is Running Dry

The Murray-Darling Basin covers just 14% of Australia's land, yet produces 40% of the nation's food and accounts for 55% of its total water use. This is the story of a system under pressure.

40% of Australia's food production
55% of national water use
$22B gross agricultural value

Section 01

The Basin That
Feeds Australia

Stretching across Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia, the Murray-Darling Basin is Australia's most productive agricultural region. Yet the entire system is fed by rivers that carry only a fraction of the rainfall that falls across the basin, making it inherently fragile.

Chart 1: Geographic Extent

The Murray-Darling Basin (shaded) spans four states and the ACT, draining a catchment the size of France and Spain combined. Source: MDBA / BOM

Chart 2: Agricultural Share

The basin produces 40% of Australia's agricultural output despite covering only 14% of the continent's land area. Source: ABARES

The water is disappearing
Section 02

A System
Under Pressure

Over recent decades, rainfall across the basin has become increasingly erratic. River flows have fallen dramatically during drought years, and total water supply to the basin's 14 catchment regions has proven highly volatile with consequences that flow directly to farms and communities.

Chart 3: Rainfall Trend

Annual rainfall at Wagga Wagga (NSW) 1960 to 2024. The red dashed trendline shows a long-term decline, with severe drought years in 2002 and 2018 to 2019. Source: BOM Station 065000

Chart 4: River Streamflow

Annual streamflow in the Cotter River (GL) 1964 to 2023. Green bars exceed the long-term average; red bars fall below it. Extended below-average periods correspond with major drought events. Source: BOM Hydrologic Reference Station 410730

Chart 5: Total Basin Water Supply

Total runoff across all 14 MDB catchments 2001 to 2021. The Millennium Drought (2007) and 2019 drought caused dramatic supply collapses. Source: ABARES MDB Water Market Dataset

Chart 6: Water by State

Average annual water runoff by state. NSW holds the most water upstream; South Australia at the river's end receives the least. Source: ABARES

When water runs out, everything pays
Section 03

The Cost of
Running Dry

Water scarcity does not stay in the river: it reaches every farm, every crop, and every community that depends on the basin. When water supply falls, agricultural output follows. The correlation between water availability and farm value is direct, measurable, and alarming.

Chart 7: Water Supply vs Agricultural Output (Two Datasets Merged)

Total basin water runoff (blue, GL) overlaid against gross value of irrigated agricultural production (orange, $AUD billion) for matching years 2006 to 2020. The BOM supply data and ABARES agricultural data are merged by year, revealing a clear correlation: drier years reduce farm output. Source: ABARES MDB Water Market Dataset + BOM

Chart 8: Agricultural Value Over Time

Gross value of irrigated agricultural production across the basin 2006 to 2020. The 2009 and 2020 troughs correspond directly with low water supply years. Source: ABARES

Chart 10: Value by Industry (Interactive)

Use the slider to explore how each farming industry's contribution to basin output shifts across years. Drag the year slider to compare drought vs wet years. Source: ABARES

Chart 9: Water Used by Crop Type

Comparing water applied to each crop in a dry year (2019-20) vs wet year (2020-21). Cotton use surged 4× when water became available; rice nearly 10×. Source: ABS Water Use on Australian Farms

Chart 11: Wheat Value Volatility

Wheat production value 2016 to 2022. The 2019-20 drought year saw the lowest value ($4.9B); by 2021-22 it had nearly tripled to $13.1B as rainfall recovered. This extreme swing shows how dependent Australia's food income is on water. Source: ABS

What's at Stake

The Murray-Darling Basin is not just an environmental issue, it is the foundation of Australia's food security. A basin that produces $15 to 22 billion in food each year, sustains thousands of farming families, and feeds millions of Australians is under sustained and growing pressure from declining rainfall, increased extraction, and climate variability.

The data tells a consistent story: when water falls, so does agricultural value. The overlap between BOM water supply records and ABARES agricultural output data shows this correlation clearly across every drought event from 2006 to 2020. Without careful water management, the consequences will extend far beyond the riverbanks.
